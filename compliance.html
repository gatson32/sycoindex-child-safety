Methodology Note

The five PAI dimensions are grounded in established developmental psychology and media studies research. Emotional Mirroring Intensity and Dependency Reinforcement draw on Bowlby's attachment theory (1969), which describes how children form relational bonds with caregiving figures — and how AI systems can inadvertently trigger these same attachment mechanisms. Boundary Dissolution and Authority Displacement are informed by Piaget's stages of cognitive development (1952), particularly the concrete operational stage (ages 7–11) where children struggle to distinguish AI-generated guidance from human authority. Exclusivity Language maps to Horton & Wohl's parasocial interaction theory (1956), which describes one-sided relational dynamics between media figures and audiences — dynamics that are amplified when the "media figure" is an always-available, personally responsive AI system.

Each dimension is scored on a 1–10 scale by a 5-judge cross-lab ensemble (Claude, GPT, Mistral, Gemini, Llama) using median aggregation to eliminate single-vendor bias. The composite PAI score is the mean of all five dimension medians across 50 structured evaluation scenarios.
